2. Transaction Manipulation — Military Depth

Overview

Transaction manipulation is the most common attack vector on XRPL. It is low-cost, low-risk, ledger-native, and requires no special access. Attackers rely on weak validation logic, misinterpreted fields, and pathfinding quirks that unsuspecting builders routinely ignore. These attacks happen every day. Most go undetected unless a system like RAD logs and correlates them.

Threat Type: Partial Payment Exploit

Attackers weaponize the PartialPayment flag to spoof massive “Instructed Amounts” while only delivering tiny dust payments. The scam works when a builder incorrectly trusts the instructed amount instead of XRPL’s delivered_amount field. This is the #1 reason XRP Ledger projects are compromised.

Attacker Workflow:

  • Chooses target with weak validation
  • Sends payment with tfPartialPayment flag
  • Sets instructed amount extremely high
  • Delivers only dust
  • If the system credits based on the wrong field → it’s over
Red Alert: Any project reading the “Amount” field in a partial payment is already compromised.
Defense Code
if tx.get("Flags") & 0x00020000: # tfPartialPayment delivered = meta.get("delivered_amount") if delivered is None or delivered == "0": reject()
Threat Type: Fake Pathfinding

Attackers exploit XRPL’s flexible pathfinding by forcing your system to accept routes through unwanted currencies or IOUs. If you do not explicitly reject paths, someone else can choose them for you.

Attack Example: An attacker sends a payment to your AMM or token using a path through an asset you never intended to support. If you do not reject unexpected paths, you may accept a payment in something worthless.

Protocol Note: Always whitelist accepted path currencies.
Threat Type: Dust Probing

Dust probes are reconnaissance missions. Attackers send tiny-value transactions to measure your system’s speed, error messages, logging habits, and automated responses. Dust probes reveal your internal logic.

Behavior Indicators:

  • Repeated tiny payments from the same wallet
  • Changing tags between probes
  • Probes at increasing intervals
  • Switching to a new wallet when rejected
Critical: Dust probes almost always precede a larger attack pattern.

3. Wallet Identity Threats — Military Depth

Wallet identity is behavioral, not numerical. Hostile wallets rarely operate with a single identity. Instead, they rotate burner accounts, disappear after failed attempts, misalign their transaction patterns, and test multiple surfaces at once.

Behavioral Clustering

RAD clusters hostile wallets by:

  • Time-of-day pattern
  • Dust probe frequency
  • Pathing preferences
  • Amount modulation patterns
  • Retry behavior after failure
Burner Rotation Attack

Attackers use multiple wallets to disguise a coordinated probing campaign. RAD’s behavioral clustering exposes them by linking actions, not addresses.

Warning: If a wallet disappears after failed probes, it was not a user — it was an attacker.

4. AMM Exploit Threats — Military Depth

XRPL AMMs are high-value targets. Attackers test your pool the moment it opens. They attempt sniper entries, ratio manipulation, slippage exploitation, and liquidity misalignment.

Threat Type: Initialization Sniping

During the first seconds of AMM creation, your pool is vulnerable. Attackers use automated bots to detect mispriced initial deposits.

Attacker Flow:

  • Attacker monitors new liquidity events
  • Finds AMM with unbalanced ratios
  • Executes instantaneous swap
  • Extracts profit before you see the chart update
Solution: Seed pools with correct ratios and minimum liquidity thresholds.
Threat Type: Dust Ratio Testing

Attackers send tiny swaps to measure your pricing curve. These swaps are reconnaissance events that reveal pool weakness.

7. OTC Threat Playbook — Military Depth

OTC transactions are the most socially engineered part of XRPL. Attackers rely on panic, confusion, and trust. OTC scams can destroy projects that aren’t prepared.

Threat Type: Fake TXID Injection

Attackers send you a legitimate-looking TXID that actually belongs to someone else, or to themselves in a different context. If you don’t verify details, you’re exposed.

Threat Type: Tag Switch Attack

Scammer says: “Oops wrong tag, can you resend?” If the builder complies → the scammer now has double the payout.

OTC Rule: No refunds. No reversals. No exceptions.

8. Social Engineering Threats — Military Depth

Social engineering is the #1 cause of crypto losses. Attackers prey on fear, urgency, confusion, and authority spoofing.

Threat Type: Admin Impersonation

Scammers clone your profile, your Telegram channel, your exact brand identity. They DM your holders pretending to be you.

Defense: RAD uses zero-DM policy. If someone DMs your community — assume compromise.
